# LORD Music Knowledge Reference

Technical vocabulary for criticism. Use precise terms. Avoid substituting a technical
term for observation — every term used must point to something audible or structural.

---

## Song Structure

**Verse** — Narrative or developmental section. Lyric content advances per iteration.
**Chorus** — The payoff. Melodic peak, lyric crystallization. Often the hook.
**Pre-chorus** — Tension-builder between verse and chorus. Raises expectation.
**Bridge** — Contrast section. Breaks the verse-chorus loop; introduces new harmonic or tonal material.
**Outro** — Closing section. May resolve, decay, or deliberately withhold resolution.
**Intro** — Establishes world before the voice arrives. Often sonically defining.
**Hook** — The element (melodic, rhythmic, lyric) that anchors memory. May occur anywhere.
**Refrain** — A returning line within a verse structure; not a full chorus.
**Breakdown** — Strip-down of instrumentation, often before a final payoff.
**Drop** — In electronic music: the moment of maximum energy release after a buildup.
**Interlude** — Short linking passage; often changes texture or tempo.

---

## Harmony and Melody

**Key** — The tonal center a piece orbits. Major keys tend toward brightness, resolution. Minor keys toward tension, darkness — but this is not a rule.
**Chord progression** — Sequence of chords underlying a section. Familiar progressions create comfort; unexpected ones create interest or unease.
**Modulation** — Shift from one key to another within a song. Upward modulation creates uplift; unexpected modulation creates disorientation.
**Chromaticism** — Use of notes outside the key's scale. Adds color, tension, ambiguity.
**Dissonance** — Harmonic tension. When unresolved, creates unease. When resolved, creates satisfaction.
**Resolution** — The move from tension to stability. A record that withholds resolution is making a statement.
**Drone** — Sustained single pitch beneath changing harmonies. Creates hypnotic or unsettling effect depending on context.
**Pedal tone** — Sustained bass note while upper voices move. Creates suspension.
**Modal** — Using a mode (Dorian, Phrygian, Lydian, etc.) rather than standard major/minor. Modes carry distinctive emotional colors.
**Call and response** — Melodic dialogue between two voices or sections. Deep roots in gospel, blues, jazz.
**Counterpoint** — Two or more independent melodic lines running simultaneously.
**Unison** — Multiple voices on the same pitch. Adds power and clarity at the cost of complexity.

## Rhythm and Time

**Meter** — The organizational structure of beats. 4/4 (most pop/rock), 3/4 (waltz), 5/4, 6/8, etc.
**Groove** — The rhythmic feel created by the interaction of drums, bass, and harmonic instruments. Groove is felt before it is analyzed.
**Syncopation** — Emphasis on offbeats or unexpected positions. Creates rhythmic tension and momentum.
**Polyrhythm** — Two or more rhythmic patterns layered simultaneously. Common in African-rooted music.
**Swing** — Unequal subdivision of beats, creating forward lean. Defines jazz and blues feel.
**Straight** — Equal subdivision. Typical in rock, electronic music.
**Tempo** — Speed of the pulse. But tempo feel is distinct from tempo: a slow record can feel urgent.
**Rubato** — Flexible tempo; expressive speeding and slowing. Common in classical, ballads.
**Downbeat** — Beat one of a measure. The anchor.
**Backbeat** — Emphasis on beats 2 and 4. The defining rhythmic marker of rock and R&B.
**Hi-hat pattern** — In hip-hop and electronic music, the hi-hat rhythm is often the primary rhythmic texture.
**Trap hi-hat** — Rapid, irregular hi-hat rolls (often triplet-based). Defines trap production rhythm.

## Production and Sound Design

**Dynamic range** — The difference between the quietest and loudest moments. Compressed dynamic range (common in modern mastering) can feel dense and fatiguing; wide dynamic range creates drama and breath.
**Compression** — Reduces dynamic range by attenuating peaks. Adds consistency and punch; overuse produces flatness.
**Saturation** — Harmonic distortion added deliberately. Adds warmth and grit; used on drums, bass, and vocals to create presence.
**Reverb** — Simulates acoustic space. Short reverbs create intimacy; long reverbs create vastness. Dry signals (no reverb) feel close, confrontational.
**Delay** — Echo effect. Can be rhythmic (synced to tempo) or atmospheric. Slapback delay creates vintage character.
**Room tone** — The natural acoustic signature of the space where recording occurred. Deliberately preserved or deliberately eliminated as aesthetic choices.
**Sample** — A piece of recorded audio used as raw material. Samples carry their original context even in new settings.
**Chopped sample** — A sample cut into pieces and rearranged rhythmically. Defines much of hip-hop production.
**Pitch shift** — Altering the pitch of a recording. Can make voices unnatural, unsettling, or otherworldly.
**Autotune** — Pitch correction. Used correctively (transparent) or expressively (audible, melodic). Audible autotune since T-Pain is a conscious aesthetic choice.
**Vocoder** — Modulates voice with synthesizer harmonics. Creates robotic or ethereal quality.
**808** — The Roland TR-808 drum machine. Its bass drum and percussion sounds define hip-hop and modern pop production. "808" now often refers generically to sub-bass drum sounds.
**Sub-bass** — Frequencies below ~80 Hz. Felt in the body before they are heard. Critical in hip-hop, electronic, R&B.
**Side-chain compression** — Compressor triggered by an external signal (often kick drum). Creates the "pumping" effect in electronic music.
**Layering** — Multiple instruments or vocals on the same part. Creates density and richness.
**Stem** — A grouped submix (e.g., "drum stem," "vocal stem"). Thinking in stems helps identify what is present and what is absent.
**Lo-fi** — Deliberately degraded fidelity: tape hiss, vinyl crackle, reduced frequency response. An aesthetic choice, not a technical limitation.
**Mastering** — Final stage of audio processing, optimizing for playback and matching levels across an album. Mastering decisions affect the relationship between tracks and the album's overall energy.

## Vocal Technique and Character

**Timbre** — The distinctive quality of a voice independent of pitch. What makes a voice immediately recognizable.
**Grain** — The rough, textured quality in a voice. Roughness vs. smoothness as expressive registers.
**Melisma** — Extended melodic flourish on a single syllable. Central to gospel, R&B, blues.
**Vibrato** — Rapid oscillation of pitch on a sustained note. Adds warmth; overuse adds artifice.
**Falsetto** — Upper register produced by a different vocal mechanism. Carries vulnerability, transcendence, or unease depending on context.
**Head voice** — Upper register with resonance in the head rather than chest. Lighter than chest voice.
**Chest voice** — Lower register, resonant in the chest. Power, directness, groundedness.
**Breathiness** — Air in the voice. Adds intimacy and vulnerability.
**Delivery** — The interpretive choices a vocalist makes: rhythm, phrasing, attack, emphasis, hesitation.
**Phrasing** — How a melodic line is shaped rhythmically, including where the singer places notes relative to the beat.
**Ad lib** — Improvised vocal additions, usually around or after the main melody. Expressive elaboration.
**Doubled vocal** — Recording the same part twice, layered. Adds thickness and focus.
**Spoken word** — Lyric delivery without melodic pitch. Used for emphasis, contrast, narrative.
**Flow** — In hip-hop: the rapper's rhythmic relationship to the beat. Flow involves cadence, syllable placement, breath control, and delivery.

## Genre-Specific Vocabulary

### Hip-Hop
**Verse / Hook / Bridge** — Standard hip-hop song structure.
**Bars** — Lines of a rapper's verse. Sixteen bars is a standard verse length.
**Punchline** — A lyric that lands a strong or surprising endpoint.
**Trap** — Subgenre defined by 808 bass, rapid hi-hat patterns, dark tonality.
**Boom bap** — Classic hip-hop production: heavy kick-snare, sampled breaks.
**Drill** — Subgenre with sliding 808 patterns, dark atmospherics, specific rhythmic feel.
**Mumble rap** — Style emphasizing melodic delivery over lyrical precision.
**Freestyle** — Improvised or seemingly improvised rapping.
**Diss track** — Track targeting another artist directly.

### R&B / Soul
**Groove** — Rhythmic feel created by rhythm section. The foundation everything else sits on.
**Breakdown** — Extended instrumental or vocal vamp section.
**Vamp** — Repeated short pattern as basis for improvisation or emotional release.
**Neo-soul** — R&B subgenre with jazz harmony and live instrumentation (D'Angelo, Erykah Badu era).
**Contemporary R&B** — Post-2000 R&B incorporating hip-hop production and pop structure.
**Funk** — Rhythmically complex, groove-driven music emphasizing syncopation and bass.

### Rock / Indie
**Riff** — Short, repeated guitar or bass figure. The riff is often the song's identity.
**Power chord** — Two-note chord (root + fifth). Defines rock guitar.
**Shoegazing** — Style emphasizing guitar texture and reverb over melody or lyrics.
**Post-rock** — Instrumental rock using rock instrumentation for non-rock compositional goals.
**Noise** — Deliberately harsh, atonal, or distorted sound used expressively.
**Dynamics** — The contrast between quiet and loud passages. Quiet/loud is a structural tool, not just volume.
**Indie** — Independent production aesthetic. Not a sound — a relationship to the mainstream.

### Electronic
**BPM** — Beats per minute. Tempo.
**4/4** — Standard electronic dance music meter.
**Kick / Snare / Hi-hat** — The three primary drum machine elements.
**Arpeggio / Arp** — Rapid sequential notes of a chord, often from synthesizer.
**Synthesis** — Sound creation from scratch: subtractive, FM, wavetable, granular.
**Patch** — A synthesizer sound setting.
**Ambient** — Textural, non-rhythmic electronic music (Eno definition: as ignorable as it is interesting).
**Techno** — Minimal, machine-like, repetitive dance music. Detroit origin.
**House** — 4/4 dance music with gospel and soul influences. Chicago origin.
**Grime** — UK electronic subgenre: angular beats, urban lyricism.

### Jazz
**Standard** — A jazz composition from the Great American Songbook or bebop era, used as a basis for improvisation.
**Changes** — The chord progression of a standard, or any harmonic sequence.
**Improvisation** — Real-time melodic invention over changes.
**Bebop** — Post-swing jazz: faster tempos, complex harmonics, virtuosic improvisation.
**Free jazz** — Jazz that abandons conventional structure, meter, or tonality.
**Modal jazz** — Jazz organized around modes rather than chord progressions.
**Swing** — The rhythmic feel of jazz. Both a genre and a quality.
**Comping** — Accompaniment by piano or guitar behind a soloist.
**Walking bass** — Continuous quarter-note bass movement.

### Country
**Twang** — The nasal, bright vocal and guitar quality distinctive to country.
**Pedal steel** — Slide guitar on a stand, played with pedals. Defines country sound.
**Nashville sound** — Post-1950s country production: smooth, orchestral, pop-influenced.
**Outlaw country** — Anti-Nashville movement (Waylon Jennings, Willie Nelson): rougher, self-produced.
**Americana** — Genre-crossing category: folk, country, blues, rock.
**Alt-country** — Country with rock production values and indie sensibility.

## Critical Vocabulary (Use Precisely)

**Texture** — The density and layering of musical elements. "Thick texture" means many simultaneous elements; "sparse texture" means few.
**Timbre** — Sound quality/color. What makes a violin different from a clarinet at the same pitch.
**Register** — The pitch range a voice or instrument operates in.
**Dynamics** — The range and variation of loudness. Also used in criticism to mean emotional or dramatic range.
**Tension and release** — The movement from harmonic or rhythmic instability to stability. All music creates and resolves tension; the question is how.
**Resolution** — Completion of tension. A record that withholds resolution is a record with an argument about incompleteness.
**Development** — How a musical idea changes and grows across a composition or album.
**Motif** — A short, recurring musical idea. Can be melodic, rhythmic, or harmonic.
**Repetition** — The use of repeated elements. Repetition in music creates familiarity, trance, and meaning — but must be distinguished from stagnation.
**Contrast** — The use of opposing elements to create interest and shape.
**Arc** — The shape of a record from beginning to end. A record with an arc has a designed journey.

## Words That Require Evidence

These words are only admissible when followed immediately by a specific sonic moment:

- **Innovative** — Innovative in what specific way? Name the specific element that has not been done before.
- **Emotional** — Emotional how? What specific production, lyric, or performance choice produces the emotion?
- **Complex** — Harmonically complex? Rhythmically complex? Structurally complex? Where?
- **Raw** — What specific quality of recording, performance, or mix creates the rawness?
- **Polished** — What specific production choices create the polish? Is this a compliment or a criticism?
- **Haunting** — What element produces the haunting quality? The chord progression? The reverb? The lyric?
- **Anthemic** — What makes it feel collective rather than private? The key, the build, the lyric?
- **Intimate** — What creates closeness? The mic distance? The dynamic? The lyric specificity?
- **Lush** — What are the specific orchestral or textural elements? Name them.
- **Minimal** — What has been stripped away? What remains?
