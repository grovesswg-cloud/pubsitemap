Jim James Returns From the Archive With Something Alive

Wowed Out is not a resurrection — it is a reclamation, and the difference is everything.

The Review — June 23, 2026
Photo: Wikipedia — Jim James / Wikipedia

The first thing you notice is the room. Not a studio room, not a treated, acoustically neutralised modern room, but something with air in it — a space that breathes and bleeds at the edges. Wowed Out, Jim James's first solo record in eight years, carries the specific warmth of music that was written for images that no longer exist. It was built from film score material, compositions that accompanied someone else's story, now orphaned from their original purpose and repurposed into something stranger and more personal. The result is an album that feels simultaneously excavated and freshly constructed, a set of songs that know something about drift and return.

The lead single 'Come Again' announces the record's intentions with disarming clarity. James's voice arrives doubled and slightly smeared, hovering over a bed of guitar that moves like slow syrup through warm glass. The production — handled largely by James himself — draws on the same deep-reverb psychedelia that defined his best work with My Morning Jacket, but stripped of that band's stadium-reaching grandeur. Where Z or Circuital wanted to fill arenas, 'Come Again' wants to fill a single room, yours specifically. The lyric circles a simple gesture — the desire to return to something, someone, some version of feeling — and James doesn't intellectualise it. He just sings it, slowly enough that you feel the weight of every word.

The film score origins are legible throughout, but they never function as a liability. What they provide, instead, is structural permission. Because these pieces were not originally built around verse-chorus-verse architecture, James is free to let them breathe past conventional shapes. Several tracks on Wowed Out feel less like songs in the traditional sense and more like climates — you exist inside them for a duration, and then they pass. The album's midsection in particular operates this way, with instrumental passages that recall the more devotional moments of Lambchop or the quieter reaches of Arthur Russell, music that resists summary because its whole point is texture over trajectory.

This is not to say the record lacks momentum. 'The Way You Carry On' demonstrates James's capacity for melodic architecture that lands with genuine weight. The track builds from a near-whispered opening into something close to gospel, his falsetto — still one of the most distinctive voices in American rock — reaching upward with what sounds like actual urgency rather than practiced performance. The production here makes a critical choice: it lets the loudness arrive late, holding back the full arrangement until the song has earned it. That patience is the mark of a songwriter who trusts his material.

The eight-year gap since Eternally Even is impossible to ignore as context, even if the album itself doesn't advertise its own gestation. That 2016 record was urgent in a different register — politically charged, restless, shaped by a specific cultural moment. Wowed Out occupies quieter ground. Where Eternally Even looked outward, this record turns inward without becoming navel-gazing. The self-examination here is conducted through feeling rather than argument. The lyrics, broadly, concern love and loss and the peculiar suspension that exists between them — territory that could easily collapse into the generic but doesn't, largely because James's imagery tends toward the concrete and strange rather than the comfortably vague. He is a writer who understands that specificity is its own form of universality.

Not every piece fully justifies its presence. There are moments in the album's second half where the dreamlike quality that makes the record so distinctive begins to work against it — where drift tips into drift-for-its-own-sake, and the lack of conventional structure becomes harder to read as intention rather than absence. A handful of tracks feel like they were included because they belonged to the archive rather than because they complete the album's argument. Wowed Out at its loosest is still pleasant, but pleasantness is not what James is reaching for, and the slight inconsistency registers.

The album's title track anchors the record's emotional centre. 'Wowed Out' — the phrase itself suggests a particular exhaustion, a being-used-up-by-wonder, and the song earns that reading. James performs it with a stillness that feels hard-won, a melody that circles without resolution, an arrangement that adds strings late and uses them wisely, as weight rather than decoration. It is the kind of song that does not announce itself as important. It simply is, and it stays with you, which is the same thing.

There is a tradition James is working within here — the tradition of rock musicians who, in their middle period, begin making music that has more in common with American Primitive or devotional folk than with the genre they came up in. It is a tradition that includes Will Oldham, Bill Callahan, Mark Kozelek at his best, and My Morning Jacket's own leader has always had more in common with that lineage than his band's arena history might suggest. Wowed Out finds him leaning fully into that current, and the results are largely right. The film score origins give the record a cinematic patience it might not have found through more conventional means, and James's voice — weathered now, more comfortable in its own register — has never sounded more naturally suited to material like this.

Eight years is a long time. The fact that this album does not feel like a comeback — does not strain toward relevance, does not calculate its audience — is itself a kind of achievement. Wowed Out sounds like a man who found something in an archive and understood, slowly, what it was actually about. The process shows, in the best sense: this is music that knows where it has been.
