The Second Act Nobody Saw Coming: Everyone Says Hi

When the bands that defined a generation dissolve, what happens to the musicians left holding the instruments? Everyone Says Hi may have the most honest answer.

The Feature — June 23, 2026
Photo: freestocks-photos / Pixabay

There is a particular kind of musician who never quite gets a clean story. Not the frontman whose solo pivot becomes a think-piece, not the songwriter who exits gracefully to produce other people's records, but the ones who were always in the room — the rhythm guitarist, the bassist, the drummer who gave those famous songs their spine — and who, when the curtain fell, simply kept playing. They are the invisible infrastructure of British indie's most celebrated era. Everyone Says Hi is made of them.

The group draws from the ruins, or rather the alumni, of some of the most significant British guitar bands of the 2000s: Kaiser Chiefs, The Kooks, The Dead 60s. On paper, that reads like a heritage act waiting to happen — a nostalgia project dressed in the clothes of something new. In practice, it is something considerably more interesting. Because what Everyone Says Hi represents is not a reunion or a repackaging. It is a genuine reckoning with what those musicians learned, what they survived, and what they still have left to say when the commercial apparatus that once surrounded them has long since moved on.

To understand the significance of this, you have to sit with the specific gravity of the era these musicians came from. British indie in the mid-2000s was a phenomenon of almost absurd cultural momentum. The Kaiser Chiefs' Employment arrived in 2005 and promptly became one of the definitive British debut albums of the decade — anthemic, sardonic, alive with working-class energy and hooks sharp enough to draw blood. The Kooks, arriving a year later with Inside In / Inside Out, occupied a different register: softer, more romantic, carried on the back of a kind of effortless melodic gift that made them immediately beloved and, in certain critical circles, immediately underestimated. The Dead 60s brought something rawer, a ska-inflected post-punk that never quite got the platform it deserved but that burned with a particular intensity. These were not peripheral moments. They shaped what indie radio sounded like, what festival culture looked like, what being young and British and in love with guitar music felt like in a specific and unrepeatable window of time.

And then, as these things do, the window closed. The cultural tectonic plates shifted. Guitar music ceded the mainstream to electronic production, to streaming-optimised sonics, to the slow dissolution of the album cycle as a meaningful cultural event. Bands contracted, members departed, projects wound down. The music that had felt so urgent became, through no fault of its own, something that occupied the past tense.

This is where the story of Everyone Says Hi gets genuinely compelling. Because the musicians who formed this group did not walk away from guitar music when it stopped being fashionable. They walked toward each other. There is something almost defiantly uncynical about that, in an era when the savvy career move would have been to pivot, to collaborate with a producer and sand down the edges, to make something that doesn't ask the listener to remember what a real room sounds like.

The new album, announced as Funny Cos It's True, arrives with a lead single called 'Don't Underestimate Yourself' — a title that functions simultaneously as a lyric, a manifesto, and, if you know the backstory, something close to a joke that also happens to be completely serious. The track moves with that specific quality of swirl that distinguishes the best British indie from its more rigid American counterparts: there is a looseness to it, a willingness to let the song breathe and rotate, to find its centre not through precision but through feel. It does not sound like a band trying to recapture something. It sounds like a band that has been in enough rooms to know exactly which corners to stand in.

What 'Don't Underestimate Yourself' does particularly well is resist the temptation toward retrospection that would sink a lesser project. The song does not wink at the past. It does not ask you to clock the pedigree and feel the warm glow of recognition. It simply exists on its own terms, which is harder than it sounds when the terms of your existence include a collective CV that spans some of the most-played records of the 2000s. The arrangement is generous without being indulgent — guitars that layer rather than compete, a rhythm section that knows the difference between anchoring a song and driving it, a melodic intelligence that suggests these musicians have spent years listening to music rather than just making it.

This matters because the alternative — the version of this project that wallows, that leans on its inherited credibility as a substitute for present-tense ideas — is everywhere. British music has no shortage of lateral formations, of supergroups that feel more like investments than bands, of projects whose primary emotional register is the comfort of the familiar. Everyone Says Hi does not feel like that. It feels like a collective of people who have genuinely processed their experiences within those famous bands — the pressure, the plateau, the slow realization that the machine was never really built for them — and arrived at something stripped of illusion.

There is a thesis here worth naming plainly: the most honest music being made right now is being made by artists who have already survived the industry's worst ideas about what success looks like. Everyone Says Hi is composed of musicians who had, by any reasonable metric, already won. They made the records. They played the venues. Their guitar lines are embedded in the cultural memory of a generation. And yet here they are, making something new without the safety net of expectation, without the algorithmic tailwind, without the label machinery that once smoothed the path. What remains when all of that is stripped away is just the music. And the music, it turns out, is the point.

The title Funny Cos It's True rewards attention. It is a phrase that belongs to a specific register of British self-awareness — the humour that is not quite deflection and not quite confession, the laugh that acknowledges discomfort without dramatising it. It is the voice of someone who has seen enough of the world to find it absurd but not enough to find it meaningless. In the context of what this band represents — musicians who spent years inside the great British indie machine and are now making music entirely outside it — there is a rueful accuracy to that framing. It is funny, the way careers end and begin and end again. It is funny, the way guitar music got written off and quietly refused to die. It is funny, the way a group of people who already had their moment are standing here with another one, refusing to call it a comeback because it never really stopped.

The announcement of UK live dates alongside the album is its own kind of statement. Touring remains the most unmediated form of musical argument — you cannot edit a room, cannot A/B test a crowd, cannot optimise a stage for the algorithm. It is where these musicians were formed and where, one suspects, they are most themselves. The live show is where the accumulated experience of everyone in this group — the festival stages, the night buses, the specific education of playing to rooms that are with you and rooms that are not — becomes an asset rather than a burden. Everyone Says Hi will walk onto those stages carrying more knowledge than most bands twice their size. The question is what they do with it.

That question is, in a sense, the central question of this entire project. Because the thing about musicians who have already had a version of success is that they no longer have the luxury of naive ambition — that particular engine, the belief that if you just get it right it will all work out, has been replaced by something more complicated and potentially more powerful. They know it can work out. They know it can also not work out, through no fault of the music, through the simple caprice of timing and culture and commercial appetite. And they are making the record anyway. They are going on tour anyway. There is a word for that, and it is not nostalgia.

When Funny Cos It's True arrives, it will be measured against the wrong things by the wrong people — against the ghost of Employment, against the echo of those Kooks chord progressions, against whatever critics decide a band in this position is supposed to be doing. The music will have to survive that misreading, and if 'Don't Underestimate Yourself' is indicative, it probably will. It has the quality of something made without permission — not rebelliously, not defiantly, but simply without waiting to be told it was allowed.

The thesis the album proposes is the thesis these musicians have been living for years: that what you make after the moment has passed is not lesser than what you made inside it. That experience does not diminish music. That the second act nobody saw coming is sometimes the most honest thing an artist ever does. The joke in the title is that it is not really a joke. Everyone says hi. The room fills up. The guitars start. The thing that never actually stopped keeps going.
