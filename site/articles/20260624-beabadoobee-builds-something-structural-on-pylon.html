Beabadoobee Builds Something Structural on Pylon

Her most ambitious and sonically fractured record yet, Pylon finds Beabadoobee locating real tension between softness and siege.

The Review — June 24, 2026
Photo: Wikipedia — Beabadoobee / Wikipedia

The first thing you hear on Pylon is not a guitar. That matters. Beabadoobee has spent four records being understood primarily as a guitarist — a bedroom-pop auteur who graduated to stadium-adjacent indie rock, whose six-string sentimentality was both her signature and, at times, her ceiling. The opening of Pylon refuses that comfortable categorisation immediately. There is a low, processed hum, something industrial and vaguely threatening, before the arrangement assembles itself around it like scaffolding going up around a building that already knows its own shape. She is announcing a recalibration.

What Pylon is, structurally and emotionally, is an album about holding weight. The title is not incidental — a pylon conducts enormous charge while standing perfectly still, and that paradox threads through almost every track here. Beabadoobee has always written with a kind of disarming directness, but this record finds her directing that candour toward harder material: isolation inside noise, tenderness under pressure, the strange loneliness of visibility. The arena tour looming over the album's release is not coincidental context. Pylon sounds like someone processing what it means to become large.

The early run of tracks establishes this tension with precision. The lead single moves with the confident momentum of someone who has learned, finally, to fully trust her own instincts in the studio — the production is dense but not cluttered, guitars layered in a way that recalls early 2000s alternative rock without ever tipping into pastiche. Lyrically, she writes in images rather than arguments, and her best lines here land with that particular bluntness that comes from having abandoned the urge to be oblique: direct address, second person, the unsettling intimacy of someone speaking to you from inside a very loud room.

The Hayley Williams collaboration is one of the album's genuine revelations. The two voices do not simply harmonise — they occupy the song differently, Williams bringing a weathered, almost liturgical quality against Beabadoobee's lighter grain, and the production widens to accommodate both without flattening either. It is the kind of feature that justifies itself on sonic terms rather than merely commercial ones. The song itself is about endurance, and there is something quietly appropriate about hearing two women at vastly different career stages singing it together, each one having arrived at the same place via entirely different roads.

Matty Healy's presence, predictably, will attract its own gravity, but the track on which he appears is stronger for refusing to centre him. His contribution sits in texture rather than structure — a countermelody, a doubled line, a kind of harmonic shadow — and Beabadoobee maintains the song's emotional ownership throughout. The production here is the album's most overtly The 1975-adjacent, lush and mid-tempo and precise, but the writing is entirely hers, and the distinction holds.

Chino Moreno's appearance is the album's most surprising and, arguably, most successful left turn. The track it anchors is the heaviest thing Beabadoobee has committed to record — distortion pushed past comfort, drums that feel like they are being played in a much larger room than the one being depicted — and Moreno's presence is less a cameo than a structural element. His voice does not soften the song. Neither does hers. They meet somewhere in the dissonance and choose to stay there. It is the moment on Pylon where her stated ambitions feel most fully realised.

Brendan Yates of Turnstile brings a corresponding kinetic energy to his collaboration, and the track functions as the album's most urgent two minutes — compact, physical, uninterested in resolution. It sits in the album's second half like a gear change that the surrounding songs absorb rather than react to, which speaks to how carefully the sequencing has been considered. Pylon is an album that has been arranged, not merely assembled.

There are moments where the ambition slightly outpaces the execution. A mid-album stretch of three tracks shares a tonal register that, across consecutive listens, begins to feel less like sustained mood and more like a plateau. The production remains intelligent throughout, but the melodic writing in this section lacks the specificity that distinguishes the record's stronger moments, and the lyrics drift occasionally toward the abstract in a way that costs them impact. These are not failures, exactly, but they are the album's softest ground, and they are noticeable precisely because the surrounding terrain is so sure-footed.

What Pylon does with remarkable consistency is refuse easy emotional resolution. Songs do not build to catharsis so much as they build to a kind of load-bearing stillness — the feeling of something large being held rather than released. This is a more sophisticated structural choice than it might appear. Pop music, and particularly the strain of melodic indie rock Beabadoobee operates in, tends toward release as its default grammar. Pylon works against that instinct deliberately, and the result is an album that lingers differently than its predecessors, that asks something more effortful from the listener in exchange for something more durable.

Her earlier records — particularly Fake It Flowers and the more polished Beatopia — were albums of feeling. Pylon is an album of structure. The feelings are still present, still specific and genuinely rendered, but they are now in service of something architectural. She is not simply expressing; she is building. The collaborators she has assembled are not ornaments but load-bearing elements, each one chosen with a clarity of purpose that reflects a songwriter who knows exactly what kind of record she is making.

That knowingness is, in the end, what elevates Pylon above the merely promising. Beabadoobee has made an album that understands its own weight, that carries it without collapsing, and that asks the people listening to stand underneath it for a while and feel what it is to be held up by something that might, if you took a piece away, come down.
