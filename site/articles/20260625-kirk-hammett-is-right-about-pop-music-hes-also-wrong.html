Kirk Hammett Is Right About Pop Music. He's Also Wrong.

Metallica's lead guitarist has spent four decades earning the right to an opinion — which makes it worth interrogating the one he just offered.

The Feature — June 25, 2026
Photo: Wikipedia — Kirk Hammett / Wikipedia

Let's begin with what Kirk Hammett actually said, and resist the temptation to bury it in context before we've looked at it plainly. Speaking in the wake of a minor cultural incident — a Taylor Swift T-shirt spotted in the Metallica camp, worn by his son, generating the kind of internet heat that passes for discourse now — Hammett told a reporter that, as far as he's concerned, contemporary songwriting and pop music is, in his word, "crap." He appended the apology himself: "Sorry for all you pop fans out there." The apology is telling. It suggests he knew what he was doing. He said it anyway. That's either honesty or provocation, and with Kirk Hammett, the distinction has always been blurrier than people assume.

What is not blurry is his standing to make the argument. Hammett has been the lead guitarist of Metallica since 1983, joining just in time to appear on Kill 'Em All and becoming one of the principal architects of what heavy metal could mean intellectually, sonically, and culturally. He is not a man who stumbled into a genre. He studied under Joe Satriani. He spent years developing a vocabulary on the instrument that was simultaneously ferocious and considered. When he plays the opening riff of "Fade to Black" from 1984's Ride the Lightning, you are hearing a musician who understood that heaviness and tenderness were not opposites. That understanding did not come cheaply.

So when Hammett says pop music is crap, the sentence carries weight that the same sentence would not carry from someone who had spent less time thinking about what music is for. And yet — and this is where the piece you actually have to write begins — the sentence is also a kind of intellectual laziness from a man who, when he is not being lazy, is one of the more thoughtful artists rock music has produced.

Metallica's career is, among other things, a prolonged argument about artistic integrity and its costs. The early albums — Kill 'Em All, Ride the Lightning, Master of Puppets in 1986 — represent a period of almost frightening creative certainty. The band knew exactly what it wanted to do and executed it with a precision that still sounds extraordinary. "Battery" opens Master of Puppets with an acoustic passage that has no business being on a thrash metal record, and it works because the band had earned the softness with the violence that followed it. These were not albums that asked for your approval. They assumed your attention and then demolished it.

...And Justice for All arrived in 1988, a record so committed to its own density and complexity that the bass guitar was effectively removed from the mix — a production choice that remains one of rock's most discussed and debated decisions. Whether it was deliberate artistic choice or engineering accident matters less than the fact that the album sounded like nothing else, and still does. It is not a comfortable record. It does not want to be. "One," its most famous track, opens with acoustic picking and ends in a machine-gun snare pattern that mirrors the lyrical horror it describes. This is songwriting. This is what songwriting can do when the people doing it have not yet begun to compromise.

Then comes the Black Album in 1991, and this is where Hammett's recent comments become most interesting to examine, because the Black Album — self-titled, co-produced with Bob Rock — was itself a pivot toward accessibility. The tempos slowed. The songs shortened. "Enter Sandman" was a hit single, played on radio stations that had never touched Metallica before. Hammett himself played the riff that opened that song to the world, a riff so deliberate and mid-paced compared to what preceded it that longtime fans experienced it as a kind of betrayal. The Black Album sold thirty million copies. It remains one of the best-selling albums in the history of recorded music.

The question Hammett's 2025 comment forces you to ask is this: was the Black Album pop? Not in genre, but in intent? It was made to reach people. It was sequenced to hold listeners who were new to the band. It was produced with a sheen that the earlier records had specifically refused. The distinction Hammett is drawing — between the craft he practices and the "crap" he perceives in contemporary songwriting — demands that he be willing to apply the same critical standard to his own catalog, and it is not clear that he is.

This is not a gotcha. This is the actual critical question that his statement opens. Because if "crap" means music made primarily for commercial effect, with structural shortcuts taken in service of accessibility, then the Black Album sits closer to that definition than Ride the Lightning does. If "crap" means something more specific — processed production, algorithmically assembled chord progressions, lyrics that have been focus-grouped rather than felt — then his point lands with more precision. But Hammett did not offer that precision. He offered a sweeping dismissal, and sweeping dismissals, however earned the voice behind them, are usually where the thinking stopped rather than where it began.

What is worth taking seriously in his sentiment is the underlying anxiety, which is real and widespread among musicians who came up in an era when the relationship between artist and song was understood as a long-term commitment. Metallica spent years on Master of Puppets. They spent years on ...And Justice for All. The writing process was iterative, argumentative, and slow. James Hetfield and Lars Ulrich would develop riff structures over months. Hammett would arrive with leads that had been practiced until they were physically internalized. The album Metallica exists because the band spent approximately a year working with Bob Rock to strip their sound down to its most essential elements. This is not how much contemporary pop is made, and Hammett knows it, and his frustration with the difference is legitimate.

But here is the counter-argument he did not make, possibly because it complicates the narrative: the history of popular music is the history of craft being underestimated by the generation that preceded it. The Beatles were dismissed by jazz musicians. Dylan was dismissed by folk purists when he went electric. Punk was dismissed by the prog rock establishment. Each of these dismissals contained a kernel of real criticism and a much larger kernel of genre protectionism. Hammett's complaint about contemporary pop does not automatically place him in that dismissive tradition, but it does make it necessary to ask whether he has genuinely engaged with what contemporary songwriters are doing before rendering judgment.

Because the argument that pop songwriting is categorically unserious requires you to account for the fact that some of the most technically and emotionally precise vocal melodic writing of the last decade has happened inside pop. It requires you to reckon with what producers like Jack Antonoff have built structurally, with what Billie Eilish and her brother Finneas O'Connell demonstrated on When We All Fall Asleep, Where Do We Go? in 2019 — an album made almost entirely in a childhood bedroom that sounds like it was made inside a human nervous system. It requires more than a single adjective.

Metallica's own later catalog is instructive here. Load and Reload, released in 1996 and 1997, were albums that confused and alienated a significant portion of the band's fanbase. They were longer, looser, influenced by Southern rock and alt-country. Hammett cut his hair. The band photographed themselves for the album artwork in ways that suggested a deliberate departure from metal iconography. The records have been substantially reappraised since then — there is real songwriting on Load, particularly in "Until It Sleeps" and "Hero of the Day" — but at the time, the critical and fan response was, broadly speaking, that Metallica had gone soft. That they had traded authenticity for commercial survival. The same language Hammett is now applying to pop was applied to those albums by people who felt that Metallica had betrayed what made them matter.

He survived that. The band survived it. And in surviving it, they made the argument by example that artistic evolution is more interesting than artistic purity. Death Magnetic in 2008 and Hardwired... to Self-Destruct in 2016 represented returns to the heavier template without repudiating the experimentation of the middle period. They were records made by a band that had accumulated enough self-knowledge to play loudly with genuine authority. The lead guitar work on "Hardwired" is Hammett's instrument reminding you what it sounds like when someone has spent four decades deciding what a note should do.

That knowledge is what makes his pop dismissal feel like an incomplete thought from a complete thinker. The version of Kirk Hammett who plays the solo on "One" — that controlled, escalating, emotionally precise piece of guitar work — would not dismiss an entire form. That version of Hammett would ask what the form is trying to do and whether it is doing it honestly. The version who makes offhand comments in interviews, still riding a minor controversy about a T-shirt his son was wearing, is the version that gives critics of rock musicians something to work with.

The Taylor Swift T-shirt, incidentally, is a perfect detail. Not because it means anything specific about what Hammett actually listens to or secretly respects, but because it illustrates the exact gap his comment fails to close. His son was wearing it. The shirt existed in the Hammett household. Music does not observe the genre distinctions its practitioners insist upon. It flows through families and generations and playlists in ways that ignore the taxonomy. Kirk Hammett knows this. He learned guitar from listening to Jimi Hendrix and Michael Schenker and Pete Townshend, musicians who were themselves dismissed by earlier arbiters of what was serious.

What the comment ultimately reveals is not that Hammett is wrong about the specific failures of algorithmic, assembly-line pop production — he is, broadly, not wrong about that — but that he reached for the broadest possible language when the most precise language was available to him. A man who helped write Master of Puppets owes the subject a more careful sentence. The work demands it. The archive demands it. And if anyone has earned the obligation to think harder about what contemporary music is and isn't doing, it is the guitarist who spent four decades proving that harder is worth it.
