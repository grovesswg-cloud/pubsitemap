Mezzanine at Twenty-Five: The Album That Predicted the Dark

Massive Attack's 1998 masterwork was never really about trip-hop — it was about dread, and dread has only become more fluent.

The Review — June 26, 2026
Photo: Wikipedia — Massive Attack / Wikipedia

There is a moment, roughly forty seconds into "Angel," when the bass arrives. Not enters — arrives, the way weather arrives, the way debt arrives. It does not announce itself. It simply occupies the room, and suddenly the room is smaller than it was before. In 1998, that moment was described as atmospheric, as cinematic, as the apotheosis of trip-hop's dark turn. Twenty-five years on, it reads differently. It reads as prophecy.

Mezzanine was not the album Massive Attack's audience expected, nor the one their critics were entirely prepared to receive. Blue Lines had invented a mood. Protection had refined it into something almost warm. Mezzanine incinerated the warmth deliberately, methodically, and without apology. Robert Del Naja, Grant Marshall, and the contributions of Andrew Vowles — before his acrimonious departure mid-session — produced something that contemporary reviewers filed under "challenging" and "abrasive" as though these were qualifications rather than achievements. They were wrong to hedge. What they were hearing was one of the most fully realized sonic environments in British music: a record that used texture the way a novelist uses subtext, to say the thing that cannot be said directly.

The received critical narrative around Mezzanine has always acknowledged its greatness while subtly diminishing its ambition. It was praised as a mood record, a headphone record, a late-night record — descriptions that are accurate but incomplete, that treat atmosphere as aesthetic decoration rather than as the primary argument. What revisitation reveals, with the clarity that only distance provides, is that Mezzanine is not a record about atmosphere. It is a record about what atmosphere conceals. Every layer of guitar — and there is more live guitar on this album than most listeners consciously register — every treated vocal, every dissonant sample loop, exists to construct a surface behind which something unnameable is moving.

"Risingson" opens the record and immediately refuses comfort. The groove is there, insistent, even seductive, but it is built from components that do not quite resolve: a bass line that circles without landing, vocals that address intimacy in the language of threat. Elizabeth Fraser's contributions throughout the album — on "Teardrop," on "Black Milk" — have been exhaustively discussed, and rightly so. Her voice on "Teardrop" remains one of the great recorded vocal performances of the 1990s, a sound that seems to arrive from outside conventional emotional registers, grieving and luminous simultaneously. But what criticism has underweighted is how Fraser's contributions function as relief rather than centerpiece. She is the light source that makes the surrounding darkness visible. Without "Inertia Creeps" — nine minutes of menace built on a guitar loop that sounds like it was excavated from somewhere underground — "Teardrop" would be a beautiful song. In context, it is a momentary reprieve from something the album is not willing to name.

What has aged poorly, if anything, is the critical tendency to locate Mezzanine within genre history rather than outside it. The trip-hop label, always a lazy convenience, has calcified into a kind of museum classification that does the record genuine damage. Trip-hop implies a particular relationship to hip-hop structure and soul-music warmth. Mezzanine has neither. Its relationship to those forms is almost entirely negative — it is defined by what it subtracts. The beats are present but submerged. The samples surface and disappear. The production by Neil Davidge and the core trio creates space not to be inhabited but to be navigated, carefully, without certainty about what occupies the shadows. Filing this record alongside Portishead's Dummy or Tricky's Maxinquaye is a geographic convenience that obscures a fundamental temperamental difference. Mezzanine is not melancholy. It is something colder and more structurally sophisticated than melancholy. It is anxiety given architectural form.

This is why the record demands reassessment now, specifically now. The cultural and psychological landscape in which Mezzanine has continued to circulate — the years since its release have constituted a masterclass in institutional anxiety, in the normalization of surveillance, in the creeping suspicion that the systems organizing daily life are not benign and not stable — is precisely the landscape the album modeled in sonic terms a quarter-century ago. This is not to claim that Del Naja and Marshall were prophets in any literal sense. It is to observe that great art carries information about the present that becomes legible only in retrospect. Mezzanine encoded the dread of late capitalism, of digital enclosure, of the particular exhaustion that accompanies the permanent state of almost-knowing that something is wrong. It did this before the vocabulary for that condition was widely available. It is more available now. The album fits the current moment the way a key fits a lock that has been waiting for it.

"Group Four" closes the original sequence — not "Exchange," which appears as a final coda — and it is perhaps the record's most undervalued track. Fourteen minutes of slow dissolution, it enacts the album's central argument in extended form: that the thing to fear is not arrival but suspension, not the crisis but the permanent anticipation of crisis. Del Naja's vocal, processed and distant, asks questions the music refuses to answer. The rhythm falls apart and reconstructs. Nothing resolves. The album ends, essentially, in the middle of something.

Contemporary criticism gave Mezzanine its proper flowers in terms of craft while underestimating its reach. What was described as a genre record was a genre-ending record — after Mezzanine, trip-hop as a coherent commercial form essentially collapsed under the weight of what Massive Attack had done to its emotional possibilities. No one could follow this without diminishing it or simply retreating. The genre did retreat. The album stayed.

Where does Mezzanine stand in history? It stands where it has always stood, which is slightly outside history, slightly ahead of it, waiting. Not warmly. It was never interested in warmth. It stands as the definitive record of a particular species of modern consciousness: awake, suspicious, unable to identify the source of the unease, unable to stop listening for it. It is not a record that rewards. It is a record that knows you.
