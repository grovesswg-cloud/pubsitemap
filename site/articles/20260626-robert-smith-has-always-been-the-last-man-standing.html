Robert Smith Has Always Been the Last Man Standing

At seventy-five, he is still finding new rooms in the house he built — and the house keeps growing.

The Feature — June 26, 2026
Photo: Wikipedia — Robert Smith (musician) / Wikipedia

There is a photograph that has circulated for decades — Robert Smith, late 1970s, in a Crawley rehearsal room, eyes ringed dark, hair a controlled disaster, holding a guitar like a man who has just remembered he owns it. He looks, as he has always looked, like someone who arrived from a slightly different dimension and decided to stay. What the photograph does not tell you, because photographs cannot tell you this, is that the person in it would outlast almost everyone else in the room — not just the room, but the era, the genre, the cultural apparatus that produced both. He would outlast punk's initial fury, post-punk's intellectual fashionability, goth's commercial zenith, Britpop's forced obsolescence of everything that came before it, and two subsequent decades of indie guitar music that borrowed from him without quite saying so. He would do all of this while remaining, stubbornly, recognizably, defiantly himself.

The news that Robert Smith appears on a new Rolling Stones single called "Divine Intervention" landed this week as a kind of category-confusing event. The Stones, who are in their own strange late chapter of institutional indestructibility, reaching across to Smith — who is now sixty-five years old and in the middle of his own improbable renaissance — the gesture should feel like nostalgia tourism. It does not feel like that. It feels, instead, like one monument acknowledging another. And the fact that it does not feel absurd is itself an argument: Robert Smith has arrived at an altitude where that kind of recognition is simply accurate.

To understand why his longevity means what it means, you have to understand what he was working against. The Cure emerged from the rubble of British punk in 1976, initially as Easy Cure, in the south of England, in a town with no particular claim on cultural importance. Their first album, Three Imaginary Boys, released in 1979, was cheerfully disowned by Smith almost immediately — he found it too glossy, too pop, not what he'd intended. This early dissatisfaction is the engine of the entire career. He was not interested in consolidating what worked. He was interested in going further in, further down, further out.

What he found, going further in, was the trilogy that defines the Cure's peak claim on art: Seventeen Seconds (1980), Faith (1981), and Pornography (1982). These three records constitute, one could reasonably argue, the most sustained sequence of controlled despair in post-punk history. Seventeen Seconds emptied the room — hollow guitars, minimal drums, production that created space as a form of emotional weather. Faith went darker still, a record about grief and extinction of belief that managed to be melodic while removing almost all conventional song satisfaction. And Pornography arrived as a near-total dissolution — drum patterns that collapsed, guitars fed through so many layers of processing they ceased to function as guitars, Smith's voice distorted and buried, lyrics that read like dispatches from the far edge of sanity. It sold badly. It has sold ever since. It is now understood, correctly, as one of the founding documents of what music can do when it decides to follow feeling all the way to its logical end regardless of commercial consequence.

The received account of what happened next is that Smith pulled back — that the singles period beginning with "The Walk" in 1983 and running through "Boys Don't Cry" re-releases and culminating in The Head on the Door in 1985 represented some kind of commercial capitulation. This is wrong, and the wrongness is instructive. Smith did not move toward the mainstream. The mainstream moved toward him. He simply changed the problem he was solving — from despair to the precise anatomy of romantic feeling — and found that a great many people had the same problem. "In Between Days" is not a lesser achievement than "A Strange Day." It is a different achievement, equally exact. The writing of perfect pop songs is not easier than the writing of post-industrial collapse. Smith proved that he could do both, which meant he belonged to no single category, which meant every category eventually had to account for him.

Kiss Me Kiss Me Kiss Me in 1987, a double album that swung from orchestral pop to guitar abrasion to Southern Gothic pastiche within a single disc, consolidated the argument that Smith operated by his own internal logic rather than genre expectation. Disintegration in 1989 was his commercial peak and, crucially, also among his most uncompromising artistic statements — a record about the terror of getting older, the obsolescence of adolescent feeling, the particular grief of realizing that intensity does not last, made with a kind of absolute production commitment that made the terror audible. "Plainsong" opens with synthesizers that function less like an introduction than like a weather system arriving. The record sold three million copies. It was not softened to do so.

Here is the counterargument, and it should be named directly: the Cure's 1990s records — Wish, Wild Mood Swings, Bloodflowers — represent a significant decline in consistent quality. Wish contains "Friday I'm in Love," a perfect radio song surrounded by records that do not quite sustain the standard the band had set for themselves. Wild Mood Swings is genuinely uncertain, a record still searching for its organizing principle. This is true. And it matters only to the extent that it reveals something real about Smith: he has never been interested in self-imitation. The lesser records are lesser because he was trying new directions, not because he was coasting. That is a different kind of failure, and it produces a different relationship to the work — even the near-misses carry the evidence of effort.

What has happened in the decade since 2010 is harder to explain but not difficult to observe. Robert Smith became a reference point so foundational that three entirely distinct generations of musicians cite him simultaneously. The influence on 1990s alternative rock — Smashing Pumpkins, Nine Inch Nails, every guitar record that used effects processing as emotional amplification — was documented at the time. The influence on early 2000s post-punk revival bands was somewhat less acknowledged but audible. What was not predicted was the influence on bedroom pop, on hyperpop, on the strain of maximalist emotional pop that has defined the late 2010s and early 2020s. When Lana Del Rey reaches for a certain kind of orchestrated yearning, she is reaching for something Smith built. When certain contemporary pop producers construct a chorus around density of feeling rather than clarity of hook, they are working in a tradition he did not invent but definitely extended.

The Olivia Rodrigo collaboration — "what's wrong with me," performed at Primavera Sound — is only strange if you have not been paying attention. Rodrigo's entire project is the emotional exactness of adolescent feeling rendered in contemporary pop production; Smith spent four albums in the early 1980s making that exactness available to anyone who needed it. The match was not a publicity stunt. It was a recognition of a shared lineage, and Smith's willingness to engage with it — to show up, to sing, to take the collaboration seriously — reveals something about how he moves through the world. He does not protect the legacy by refusing to touch anything new. He protects it by bringing the same seriousness to every room he enters.

The Rolling Stones collaboration follows the same logic, but with a different charge. This is two institutions acknowledging each other across a table. The Stones have been playing with time for years — Hackney Diamonds, released in 2023, was by most reasonable critical accounting their strongest record in three decades, proof that longevity does not require irrelevance. That they reached for Smith, specifically, for a song called "Divine Intervention," is a kind of appointment. They are not reaching for a nostalgia act. They are reaching for someone who has maintained, across nearly fifty years of recorded work, a consistent claim on emotional seriousness. The title itself — divine intervention, the idea of something breaking through from outside the natural order — could be a description of what Smith's voice does in a room. It does not belong to any decade. It interrupts.

Songs of a Lost World, released in 2024 and the Cure's first album in sixteen years, is the piece of evidence the critical establishment has been slow to fully reckon with. It is, without much question, one of the major records of the decade so far — a late-career work that does not soften what age has done to the material but makes the age itself part of the argument. The production, layers of guitar and strings building architectures that resolve into something like grief made habitable, returns to the emotional territory of Pornography and Disintegration without quoting from either. It sounds like a man who knows exactly how much time has passed and refuses to pretend otherwise. "Alone," the opening track, is nine minutes long and contains more earned feeling per minute than most albums contain across their entire running time. The record did not perform as a nostalgic exercise. It performed as evidence that Smith's interior life is still genuinely interior — still producing material that could not have been made by anyone else, at any other time.

The photograph from Crawley in the late 1970s cannot tell you this. What it shows is a young man who looks like he arrived from somewhere else. What it cannot show is where he was going: through four decades of records that refused to simplify, through genres that claimed him and movements that tried to contain him, through collaborations that would have looked baffling on paper and landed as inevitable in execution, through a silence of sixteen years and a return that justified the wait. The house he built keeps growing new rooms. And he is still the last man standing in each of them, exactly as dark-eyed, exactly as precise, exactly as unwilling to do the easier version of the thing.

Divine intervention, the song says. The description is more accurate than it might appear.
